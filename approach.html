My approach

Start with WHY

A good product person thinks about the WHYs before they think about the WHATs. They tirelessly strive to uncover actual, real problems and customer needs before they start thinking about solutions. Anybody can come up with an idea for a cool feature. This is exactly why overall appx. 80% of software features are never used. Product management is not about prioritising features. It’s about solving the right problems. In fact, a product person can sometimes solve a customer problem by removing features!

Surfing is cool, but it’s all about scuba diving

A good product person dives deep. They don’t roam the surface. The only way to make the right decisions about the present and future evolution of your product is by going deep. By that I mean getting an intimate understanding of your customers’ view on the world and the problems that THEY are trying to solve. Then you need to know the nitty gritty details of how your product works, so that you are completely aware of what problems you can solve. Then it’s about striking a balance. Knowing where we are at the moment and where our customers need to be, where should we take our product, so that everyone is at a better place down the road? You can’t answer that question without going deep.

Be curious, listen and connect the dots

Who says that the product person is the only one who comes up with the right problems to solve? A good product person speaks to such a diverse range of people regularly, discusses so many topics with them, that inevitably those people end up sharing legitimate problems that are worth solving. The skill of the product person is in creating such conversations and really, really listening. The skill is in being able to understand what you hear and being able to tell a legitimate problem from all of the feature suggestions you are going to receive. Finally, you need to connect the dots. Good product decisions aren’t rocket science (in most cases). Typically they are just a creative interpretation of what everybody already knows.

Prioritize

You can’t make all of the people happy all of the time, right? You can’t solve all problems at once. You gotta say “no”. Invest some time in learning how to say “no” so that people keep wanting to work with you even after having heard that “no” a few times in a row. The key here is how you think of prioritisation, how clear your prioritisation principles are and how well you can explain them to your audience.

Give to receive

Regardless of where in the hierarchy your product role is, you need to know your craft as well as you could. The quality of your work directly helps people in achieving their goals. There are so many things that a product person can do to help an organisation get to a better place, to make work meaningful. Know your craft, experiment, try new things, be curious, explore alternatives, roll up your sleeves, find problems and work with others to find the right solutions. Show, don’t tell and give to receive.
